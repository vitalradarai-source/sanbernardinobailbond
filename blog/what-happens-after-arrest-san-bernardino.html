The Arrest — What Happens First

San Bernardino is the county seat of the largest county by area in the United States, and its criminal justice system processes a high volume of arrests. When someone is arrested in San Bernardino, they are typically booked at the Central Detention Center on Rialto Avenue — the primary intake facility for the San Bernardino County Sheriff's jail system.

When San Bernardino Police Department makes an arrest in San Bernardino, the officer must inform the person of the reason for the arrest and read them their Miranda rights. The arrested person is then transported to Central Detention Center for booking. During transport, the person has the right to remain silent — and exercising that right is almost always the wisest choice.

Under California law (Penal Code Section 851.5), every person arrested has the right to make at least three completed phone calls immediately upon booking, or no later than three hours after arrest. These calls are free if they are within the local dialing area. Use them wisely — call a family member, a bail bondsman, and if possible, an attorney.

The Booking Process at Central Detention Center

Booking is the administrative process that creates the official record of the arrest. At Central Detention Center, booking typically involves the following steps:

  • Personal information: Full name, date of birth, address, and physical description are recorded
  • Fingerprinting: Electronic fingerprints are taken and checked against state and federal databases
  • Mugshot: A photograph is taken for the booking record
  • Background check: Officers search for outstanding warrants, prior convictions, and parole or probation status
  • Property inventory: Personal belongings are cataloged and stored until release
  • Medical screening: A brief health assessment is conducted
  • Charges recorded: The specific charges (with California Penal Code sections) are formally entered into the system

The Central Detention Center (CDC) in San Bernardino is one of the busiest booking facilities in the Inland Empire. It serves as the primary intake point for arrests throughout the city and surrounding areas. San Bernardino County has its own bail schedule, which differs from LA and Orange County amounts.

How to Post Bail and Get Released

Once bail is set, there are several ways to secure release:

  1. Bail bond (most common): You pay a licensed bail bondsman 10% of the total bail amount. The bondsman posts the full bail with the court as a surety bond. On a $50,000 bail, you pay $5,000. This 10% premium is the bondsman's fee — it is set by California law and is non-refundable.
  2. Cash bail: You pay the full bail amount directly to the court. This money is returned (minus administrative fees) when the case concludes, but most families do not have tens of thousands of dollars available on short notice.
  3. Own recognizance (OR): The judge releases the person on a written promise to appear, with no bail required. OR release is typically reserved for low-level, nonviolent offenses where the defendant has strong community ties.

After the bail bond is posted at Central Detention Center, the release process begins. Release can take an additional 2 to 6 hours depending on facility volume and staffing. The total time from arrest to release is typically 6 to 14 hours when a bail bondsman is involved from the start.

What Happens After Release

Release on bail is not the end of the legal process — it is the beginning. After being released from Central Detention Center, the defendant will have several obligations:

  • Arraignment: The defendant must appear in court (typically at San Bernardino Justice Center) within 48 hours of arrest (excluding weekends and holidays). At arraignment, formal charges are read, and the defendant enters a plea.
  • Bail conditions: The court may impose conditions such as no-contact orders, travel restrictions, drug testing, or check-ins with a pretrial services officer.
  • Future court dates: Missing any court appearance can result in a bench warrant, bail forfeiture, and additional criminal charges (failure to appear under PC 1320).
  • Attorney consultation: If you have not already hired a defense attorney, do so before arraignment. A public defender will be appointed if you cannot afford private counsel.

The bail bond remains in effect for the duration of the case — which can last months or even years for serious charges. As long as the defendant appears at every required court date, the bond stays active and no additional money is owed to the bondsman.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does booking take at Central Detention Center?

Booking at Central Detention Center typically takes 2 to 8 hours depending on how busy the facility is, the complexity of the charges, and whether background checks reveal additional warrants. During this time, you can call Angels Bail Bonds to start the paperwork so we are ready to post bail the moment booking is complete.

Can I bail someone out right away after an arrest in San Bernardino?

Yes. Once booking is complete and bail has been set according to the San Bernardino County bail schedule, a bail bond can be posted immediately. There is no mandatory waiting period in California. The sooner you call a bail bondsman, the sooner the release process begins.

How much does a bail bondsman charge in San Bernardino?

In California, the standard bail bond premium is 10% of the total bail amount. This rate is set by the California Department of Insurance and is the same statewide. On a $50,000 bail, you would pay $5,000 to the bondsman. This fee is non-refundable — it is the cost of the bondsman's guarantee to the court.

What are my three free phone calls after arrest?

Under California Penal Code Section 851.5, every person arrested has the right to make at least three completed phone calls — free of charge if local — immediately upon booking or no later than three hours after arrest. You can call a family member, an attorney, or a bail bondsman. Use one of these calls wisely.

What happens if bail is set too high?

If bail seems excessive, a defense attorney can request a bail reduction hearing at arraignment. The judge will consider factors like community ties, employment, criminal history, and flight risk. Under the Eighth Amendment, bail cannot be set at an amount designed to punish — it must be reasonably related to ensuring the defendant appears in court.
