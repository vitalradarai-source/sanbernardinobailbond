The First 3 Things to Do Right Now

If someone you care about was just arrested in San Bernardino, take a breath — then take action. The faster you move, the faster they come home. Here's what to do right now:

  1. Find out where they're being held. Ask the arresting officer, call the local police department (San Bernardino PD), or search the county inmate locator online. In San Bernardino County, you can also call the jail information line directly.
  2. Get the booking number. This is the key identifier that speeds up everything. If booking isn't complete yet, the jail can give you an estimated completion time. Under California Penal Code 851.5, the arrested person gets at least three free phone calls within three hours of booking — one of those calls should be to you or a bail bondsman.
  3. Call a bail bondsman immediately — (626) 478-1062. You don't have to wait for booking to finish. A licensed bail agent can start the paperwork while the jail is still processing, which means the bond is ready to post the moment bail is set.

Critical timing tip: Central Detention Center is the primary intake for San Bernardino County — release takes 4 to 10 hours after bail is posted. The sooner you call, the sooner they're out.

3 Ways to Get Someone Out of Jail in San Bernardino

There are three ways to get someone released from jail in California. Each has trade-offs:

Option 1: Bail Bond (Most Common)
Pay a licensed bail bondsman 10% of the total bail as a non-refundable premium. The bondsman posts a surety bond for the full amount. This is what most families choose because you don't need tens of thousands in cash. Call (626) 478-1062 to start.

Option 2: Cash Bail
Pay the full bail amount directly to the court or jail. If bail is $50,000, you pay $50,000 in cash (or cashier's check). The money is refunded after the case ends (minus fees) — but most families simply don't have this kind of cash available during an emergency.

Option 3: Own Recognizance (OR) Release
The defendant is released with a written promise to appear — no bail required. This is only granted for low-risk defendants charged with minor offenses, and a judge must approve it. You can't request this yourself — it's decided at arraignment.

For most families, a bail bond is the only realistic option. Here's why: if bail is $50,000, coming up with $5,000 (the 10% premium) is difficult but possible — especially with a payment plan. Coming up with $50,000 in cash on short notice is nearly impossible for most people.

How Long Does Release Take from Central Detention Center?

Release times vary significantly depending on the facility:

  • Small city jails (Hermosa Beach PD, Tustin PD, La Palma/Cypress PD): 1 to 4 hours after bail is posted
  • Medium facilities (Garden Grove PD, West Covina PD, Industry Station): 3 to 8 hours after bail
  • Large county jails (Twin Towers, Men's Central Jail, Theo Lacy, Central Detention Center): 6 to 14 hours after bail

Why the speed matters: If your loved one is arrested and booked at a small city jail, posting bail BEFORE they're transferred to a county facility can save hours — sometimes an entire day. Once someone enters the county system, they're in a much larger queue.

Weekend and holiday arrests: Booking and release processing runs 24/7, but weekends and holidays are typically slower due to reduced staffing. If someone is arrested on a Friday night, expect longer processing times. This is another reason to call a bail bondsman immediately — we can have the bond ready to post the moment bail is set, minimizing wait time.

What Information Do You Need to Post Bail?

When you call a bail bondsman, have as much of this information as possible:

  • Defendant's full legal name (as it appears on their ID)
  • Date of birth
  • Booking number (if available — the jail assigns this during booking)
  • Which jail or station they're being held at
  • Charges (if known)
  • Your relationship to the defendant (cosigners are usually family members or close friends)

Don't worry if you don't have all of this — a bail bondsman can look up most of it using just the defendant's name and date of birth. The important thing is to call as soon as possible rather than waiting until you have every detail.

For the cosigner: You'll need a valid government-issued ID and a way to pay the premium (cash, credit card, or check). If you're signing electronically, you'll need access to email or a smartphone.

Frequently Asked Questions

How fast can I get someone out of jail in San Bernardino?

The total time from your first call to release depends on the facility. At smaller city jails, the entire process can take 4 to 6 hours. At large county facilities, expect 8 to 14 hours. Calling a bail bondsman immediately — before booking is even finished — is the fastest path to release.

Can I bail someone out at night or on weekends?

Yes. Bail can be posted 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, including holidays. Angels Bail Bonds answers the phone around the clock at (626) 478-1062. Jails process bail and releases at all hours, though weekend processing may be slower.

Do I have to go to the jail to post bail?

No. Your bail bondsman goes to the jail to post the bond on your behalf. You can sign paperwork remotely (electronically or by fax) and pay by phone. You only need to go to the jail to pick up your loved one when they're released.

What if I can't afford the 10% bail bond premium?

Angels Bail Bonds offers payment plans for families who can't pay the full 10% upfront. Call (626) 478-1062 to discuss your situation — we work with families in all financial circumstances and can often find a way to get your loved one released today.

What happens if the person I bail out doesn't go to court?

If the defendant misses a court date (Failure to Appear), a bench warrant is issued and the bail bond enters forfeiture. The bondsman has 180 days to locate the defendant. As the cosigner, you may become financially responsible for the full bail amount. Always make sure the defendant knows their court dates.
